Project Summary

Problem Statement

Urban growth is often mapped in terms of where development occurs, but this rarely explains what is lost and what environmental costs are produced. New built-up areas may replace green space, encroach on water-sensitive zones, and increase local heat exposure.

This application addresses that gap by identifying where urban expansion has taken place across London, what land has been replaced, and whether these changes are associated with uneven environmental costs across boroughs.

End User

This application is designed for urban planners, local authorities, environmental teams, and community organisations. These users need accessible spatial evidence to understand whether recent urban growth has generated disproportionate environmental costs and where intervention may be needed.

The tool supports this by allowing users to identify high-cost growth areas, compare boroughs, and explore how development relates to green space loss, water-edge pressure, and heat exposure, without requiring specialist GIS expertise.

Data

The project uses multiple datasets available in Google Earth Engine. Dynamic World is used to detect land cover transitions and identify newly built-up areas. Sentinel-2 imagery is used to calculate NDVI as an indicator of vegetation change, while Landsat 8/9 is used to estimate land surface temperature and heat penalty.

JRC Global Surface Water is used to identify stable water bodies and potential encroachment on sensitive water-edge environments. London borough boundaries are used to aggregate results and enable comparison across the city.

Methodology

The analysis begins by identifying newly built-up areas through land cover change detection. For these expansion areas, a series of environmental indicators are calculated, including land replacement type, green space loss, NDVI reduction, heat penalty, and proximity to water edges.

These indicators are aggregated at borough level to enable comparison across London. A composite growth cost index is then constructed to summarise the environmental cost of urban expansion, highlighting spatial inequalities in development impact.

Interface

The interface combines an interactive map, control panel, and summary outputs to support a simple analytical workflow. Users can switch between layers showing new built-up areas, land replacement types, NDVI loss, heat penalty, and composite growth cost.

They can select a borough from a dropdown menu or click directly on the map to view local summaries. Supporting charts provide comparative insights, allowing users to move from spatial exploration to analytical understanding.

How to Use This Application

Start with the interactive map to explore where urban growth has taken place and how environmental cost varies across London.

Then use the borough ranking chart to identify which areas show the highest relative environmental cost. Finally, examine the land-replacement chart to understand what kinds of land are most frequently being converted into built-up surfaces.

Together, these components help users move from spatial observation to comparative interpretation.

How the Cost Index is Calculated

The environmental growth cost index combines multiple indicators derived from Google Earth Engine:

  • vegetation loss and NDVI decline
  • heat exposure associated with new built-up land
  • pressure on water-adjacent land
  • land-cover transitions into built surfaces

Each indicator is normalised and aggregated to produce a composite score at borough level. This allows comparison across boroughs while capturing multiple dimensions of environmental impact.

This index is intended as a comparative diagnostic measure rather than a precise absolute measure of environmental damage.

In contrast, inner boroughs tend to show lower cost, indicating that growth may be occurring on already urbanised land.

What the Results Suggest

Environmental cost is not evenly distributed across London.

Higher-cost boroughs are not simply those with more development, but those where growth is more strongly associated with vegetation loss, heat exposure, and ecological disruption.

The combined use of spatial layers and borough-level charts shows that urban expansion can produce very different environmental outcomes depending on where it occurs and what land it replaces.

Why This Matters

By highlighting where urban growth is associated with ecological trade-offs, this application supports more informed planning decisions that prioritise not only development quantity but also environmental quality.

It helps shift attention from simply measuring how much development has occurred to understanding how and where that development may generate greater environmental cost.

Behind the Application

This application is designed for planners, local authorities, environmental teams, and community groups who need accessible spatial evidence on where recent urban growth may be associated with higher environmental cost.

It uses Google Earth Engine data including land-cover transitions, NDVI, land surface temperature, and water-related indicators to support borough-level comparison and help users identify where further investigation or intervention may be needed.

Limitations

This analysis relies on satellite-derived indicators which may not capture all fine-scale urban changes.

Uncertainty may arise from land-cover classification, temporal resolution, and the aggregation of multiple indicators into a single comparative index.

The growth cost index should therefore be interpreted as a diagnostic and comparative measure rather than an absolute representation of environmental impact.
